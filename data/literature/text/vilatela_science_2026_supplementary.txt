     Materials and Methods
     Materials
          Commercial CNTs fibers from DexMat (USA) produced by liquid crystal spinning were used.
     The fiber is composed of highly aligned DWCNTs and spun as reported elsewhere (1). The
 5   samples were supplied after annealing at 500 °C for 5 hours to remove residual acid used for
     spinning, which is itself a dopant. All tests were done on CNT fiber filaments, either those supplied
     in such format or from multi-filament strands. The outer diameter of the constituent DWCNTs
     determined from image analysis of TEM micrographs of FIB-milled cross-sectional lamella has a
     log-normal distribution with mean of 2.1 nm.
10
     Intercalation Process
           The two-zone vapor transport technique (18) was employed for synthesizing the carbon
     nanotube intercalation compound (CNTIC). Sample preparation was done in a glove box with an
     inert argon atmosphere and relative humidity below 0.05%. Pristine (undoped) samples are loaded
15   into a ceramic boat at one end of a sealed quartz ampoule. A separate ceramic boat containing a
     mixture of 20 mg N-chlorosuccinimide (NCS) and 70 mg anhydrous AlCl3 powders is placed at
     the opposite end of the ampoule. NCS is used as a chlorinating agent, thermally decomposing to
     create a Cl2 atmosphere (35). This results in an AlCl4- rich atmosphere, easing vapor transport of
     the intercalant species, and their introduction and stabilization inside the CNT interstices. The
20   pressure in the ampoule is reduced to ~10-2 mbar before sealing. A heat gradient is applied to avoid
     condensation on the surface of the sample, keeping the sample zone at 120 ºC and the reagent zone
     at approximately 110ºC. The system is held under these conditions for 24 hours. Afterwards,
     residual gases are removed, and the samples are retrieved and stored under an inert atmosphere for
     subsequent analysis and tests.
25
     Characterization
          Intercalant mass fraction was calculated from gravimetric measurements on fiber samples
     with lengths of 30 cm, weighed inside a glove box. The gravimetric data was in agreement with
     values from vibroscopic linear density measurements obtained with a Favimat tensile tester at 0.4
30   cN/tex pretension. The gravimetric linear density values were used for calculation of volumetric
     density and specific properties. To calculate the volume fraction of intercalant, we assume
     perfectly aligned fibers of closed-packed monodisperse CNTs, with skeletal density of 1.61 ±
     0.41 𝑔/𝑐𝑚3.
          Fiber diameter distributions were obtained from image analysis of SEM micrographs,
35   assuming the fibers have a circular cross-section. Further diameter measurements were performed
     by optical microscopy of fiber cross-sections. The mean diameter was used for calculations of
     electrical conductivity and mechanical properties.
          WAXS patterns were obtained using an Anton Paar SAXSPoint5.0 machine. The
     measurements were performed in triplicate, exposing the vertically oriented fibers to the 0.15 nm-
40   wavelength beam for 20 minutes. A 1 mm-diameter X-ray beam was used. The sample-detector
     distance was 62.2 mm. The data were treated using the associated software SAXSAnalysis for
     background removal. The two-dimensional scattering data was reduced to one-dimensional
     profiles through radial or azimuthal integration.
     Raman spectra were collected with 532 nm, 633 nm and 785 nm laser lines, using 0.5% power
45   with 3 accumulations and 10 s exposure. The laser polarization was kept parallel to the fiber axis
     to increase scattering intensity. To avoid exposure to humidity, some fibers were observed in a
     dedicated Raman cell filled with argon. The G band was fitted with three Lorentzian peaks and a

     Breit-Wigner-Fano (BWF) peak. Other combinations were tested, but this one produced the best
     fit using four peaks. Charge transfer per carbon atom (𝑓𝐶,𝑅𝑎𝑚𝑎𝑛 ) was calculated using Δ𝐺𝑜+ =

           The fiber cross-sections were produced by focused ion (FIB) beam milling using a FIB-FEG
 5   SEM Helios Nanolab 600i (FEI) and used for corresponding elemental mapping by Energy
     Dispersive X-ray Spectrometry (EDX). Lamellae for transmission electron microscopy (TEM)
     from both the intercalated and pristine CNTs fibers were fabricated using a dual beam Thermo
     Fisher Scientific Helios 600. In the case of the intercalated fibers, due to their sensitivity to
     humidity, these FIB lamellae were prepared and transferred under controlled (argon and vacuum)
10   atmospheres. The transfer was done using a Thermo Fisher Scientific Clean Connect Sample
     Transfer System, filled with argon, to transfer the samples from the FIB to the TEM air-free
     transfer holder (Gatan double-tilt 648 model) within a glove box, also filled with argon. Scanning
     TEM imaging was performed in a doble-aberration-corrected Thermo Fisher Scientific Titan Cube
     working at 80 kV. SEM micrographs of the lamella are included in Figure S13. EDX maps of
15   cross-sectional lamella were obtained by multi-frame STEM-EDX acquisition. Each frame was
     spatially aligned using its corresponding high-angle annular dark field (HAADF) intensity to
     improve spatial resolution. The resulting summed EDX spectrum image was analyzed by spatial
     and spectral re-binning to provide sufficient signal-to-noise ratio. Principal component analysis
     (PCA) was used as a denoising routine, and relative atomic quantification was performed.
20         Electrical measurements consisted of 4-probe electrical resistance measurements on fibers of
     5 centimeters in length, as well as 2-probe measurements on samples at different gauge lengths (1
     – 3 cm) to obtain resistivity from the corresponding linear regression. The fibers were inserted
     between two frames with pre-made contacts of copper foil, held together under slight pressure.
     Values of conductivity were calculated using the fiber mean diameter to calculate their cross-
25   sectional area, assuming a circular cross-section. High fiber conductivity values are consistently
     and reproducibly obtained when performing intercalations and electrical measurements on
     different occasions. Error bars are determined from the standard deviation in experimental data.
     For measurements on a reduced number of samples, error bars are calculated based on error
     propagation.
30         Tensile tests were performed on 10 mm gauge length fibers using a Favimat tensile tester at
     1.0 mm/min speed. The fibers were tested after 1-3 hours of exposure to ambient (humid)
     conditions and thus reflect a lower bound of tensile properties. The specific values of strength and
     modulus in GPa/SG units were calculated using the gravimetric measurements performed in a
     glovebox. Standard deviation for tensile strength was determined from the spread in the
35   experimental values.
           X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were performed in a Kratos Axis
     SUPRA spectrometer employing a monochromatic Al Kα (1486.6 eV) X-ray source. Survey
     spectra were obtained with an energy step of 1 eV, 160 eV analyzer pass energy and resolution of
     0.45eV, using the charge neutralizer during spectral acquisition. The spectra were analyzed using
40   CASA XPS software and calibrated with the binding energy of the C 1s peak at 284.5 eV. The
     data were analyzed using a Shirley function for the background, and a symmetrical Voigt profile
     parameterized as a Gaussian-Lorentzian function (GL 30 (Gaussian-Lorentzian mix)). In the signal
     fitting process, all contributions are included: both the one corresponding to lifetime broadening
     (core-hole lifetime —the natural linewidth of the photoemission process, which is inversely
45   proportional to the core-hole lifetime), which accounts for the Lorentzian contribution, and all
     other contributions, including the experimental one (experimental resolution and sample
     inhomogeneity (e.g., variations in binding energy due to charging or different chemical

     environments)), which provides the Gaussian profile. Ultraviolet photon spectroscopy (UPS)
     measurements were carried out using He I excitation (21.2 eV) and recorded with a constant pass
     energy of 5 eV in the ultrahigh vacuum (UHV) chamber of the XPS instrument.
           Three different conditions of sample exposure to humidity were studied. Fibers kept in a
 5   glovebox (RH < 0.05%). Samples transferred from the glove box to and kept in a metal chamber
     with mean humidity of 7% — measured with a portable hygrometer —, kept constant through flow
     of dry air. Samples kept in ambient conditions, which correspond to a mean RH of approximately
     44%.

     on intercalated samples embedded in Chipquick EGS10C-20G electronic-grade commercial

     Computational Details
          All density functional theory (DFT) calculations performed in this work were carried out with
     the 7.1 version of the Quantum Espresso package (36, 37). The electron exchange-correlation was
     approached by the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof
20   (PBE) exchange-correlation functional (38). Grimme’s D3 correction (39) was used to account for
     dispersion interactions. Brillouin zone calculations were performed using a Marzari-Vanderbit-
     DeVita-Payne “cold” smearing of 0.035 Ry (40).
          The electron density of the inner core electrons was approximated using the standard solid-
     state pseudopotentials (SSSP) (41). For all relaxation tasks (both variable-cell and geometry
25   relaxation), we first performed a calculation with the SSSP efficiency library and then a second
     relaxation with the SSSP precision library. The plane-wave basis was expanded to a cutoff energy
     equal to the highest one among the suggested values for C, Cl, and Al (45 Ry for the efficiency
     step and 100 Ry for the precision step). The Monkhorst-Pack k-points were sized (1 x 1 x 6) for
     the efficiency step and a denser (converged) grid of (2 x 2 x 6) was used for the precision step.
30        Atomic charges were computed in order to analyze the charge transfer in the system of
     nanotube and intercalant. For this, we employed the Bader program, developed by Yu and Trinkle
     (42), which uses the Bader definition of an atom to predict the electron density partition. In this
     case, PAW pseudopotentials were used for obtaining the electron density used as input by the
     Bader program. Other specific computational details are described below.
35
     Nanotube
          The carbon nanotube unit cell was constructed considering a double-walled CNT (DWCNT)
     with a (9,9) CNT and a (14,14) CNT as inner and outer CNTS, respectively. A unit cell with a
     (14,14) single-walled carbon nanotube (SWCNT) was also simulated. Five CNT unit cell layers
40   were considered in the z direction. Variable-cell relaxation calculations were carried out to obtain
     the optimal lattice parameters and atomic positions. The optimized DWCNT unit cell is presented
     in Fig. S1.

     Nanotube and Intercalant
45       The optimized nanotube unit cell was used as the base to construct the unit cell of the
     DWCNT and intercalant unit cell. The intercalant molecules were positioned in the biggest

     interstices between adjacent nanotubes as suggested by our experimental data. The composition of
     the intercalant and its initial geometry was the result of analyzing both experimental results and
     DFT calculations. The DWCNT cell simulated corresponds to an intercalant mass fraction of 10.9
     wt.%, AlCl4 molar fraction of 0.0086, and stoichiometric formula C115AlCl4.
 5         To assess different possible intercalants, we relied on a simplified system model to increase
     computational efficiency by considering an optimized (14,14) SWCNT matching the outer layer
     of the DWCNT (16.7 wt.% of intercalant, corresponding to 0.014 molar fraction or C70AlCl4
     stoichiometry). Our first educated guess of the intercalant is shown in Fig S2A. We first considered
     Al2Cl6 as intercalant. After preliminary non-converging DFT relaxation calculations, we observed
10   that the Al2Cl6 tended to dissociate into two AlCl3 molecules. Based on this result, we refined the
     initial geometry of our intercalants considering two AlCl3 instead of a single Al2Cl6 molecule,
     while respecting the geometric parameters (distances, angles) suggested by our experiments (Fig.
     S2B). Following prior studies in the literature and additional conclusions drawn from our
     experiments, we concluded that the presence of AlCl4- was more probable than that of Al2Cl6 (43).
15   Hence, we tried two new different intercalants with additional Cl atoms: Al2Cl7- (Fig. S2C) and
     (AlCl4-)2 (Fig. S2D).
           Geometry relaxations were carried out to obtain the optimized structure of the intercalant. For
     all these calculations, all atoms of the nanotube were kept fixed since experiments suggest that
     there is no modification of the nanotube structure after intercalation. After geometry relaxations
20   we computed energies of intercalation (𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑡 ) for both Al2Cl7- and (AlCl4-)2 to determine which of
     the two was the most probable intercalant in the system:

                      𝐴𝑙 𝐶𝑙7−
                     𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑡2     = 𝐸𝑆𝑊𝐶𝑁𝑇+2𝐴𝑙2𝐶𝑙7− − 𝐸𝑆𝑊𝐶𝑁𝑇 − 4𝐸𝐴𝑙𝐶𝑙3 − 𝐸𝐶𝑙2 = 6.89 𝑒𝑉
                    (𝐴𝑙𝐶𝑙4− )2
                   𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑡        = 𝐸𝑆𝑊𝐶𝑁𝑇+2(𝐴𝑙𝐶𝑙4−)2 − 𝐸𝑆𝑊𝐶𝑁𝑇 − 4𝐸𝐴𝑙𝐶𝑙3 − 2𝐸𝐶𝑙2 = 6.36 𝑒𝑉
25
          From this, we found that the 𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑡 of AlCl4- (6.36 eV/unit cell) was lower than that of Al2Cl7-
     (6.89 eV/unit cell). Hence, we consider AlCl4- to be the most probable intercalant in our system
     and further simulations were done exclusively for such intercalant.

     Density of states
     DFT calculations provide relative energy values as observables. This also holds for the Fermi level,
     Ef. Hence, the comparison of pristine and intercalated materials is in terms of the relative energy
     values of the DOS with respect to the computed Ef (E - Ef). By doing so, the position of the Ef in
10   the plot of DOS for both systems can be determined (Figure S3). As such, the shift of the Ef from
     the pristine material relative to the intercalated one can be approximated by taking the difference
     between the energies of the minima of their DOS (the minimum of the DOS of the pristine material
     corresponds to its Fermi level). This value was found to be -0.44 eV. Therefore, if we assume that
     the Fermi level of the pristine material is approximately -4.50 eV, the Fermi level of the
15   intercalated CNT should be located at -4.94 eV (which does not correspond to the minimum of its
     DOS).

     Comparison with Overhead Cables and their Related Materials
           The type of cables used in overhead power transmission lines are aluminum conductor steel-
     reinforced cables (ACSR). In those cables, the aluminum alloy (such as 1350-H19) is used for the
     outer strands, and the core is made of galvanized steel. The prime function of aluminum strands is
 5   current conduction to ensure efficient electricity transmission with minimal energy loss, whereas
     steel strands in the core offer mechanical reinforcement to support the cable weight for long spans
     between power pylons, including under harsh weather conditions (strong wind, ice buildup,
     temperature shifts).
           Table S7 compares the properties of the intercalated CNT fiber in this work with ACSR
10   materials. The comparison also includes other emerging cables reinforced with carbon fiber
     composite core (ACCC) materials.
           It is also instructive to make a comparison of properties of prototypical cables. As an example,
     we take a standard ACSR cable of 30/7 stranding commonly presented in the market (USA, China,
     Saudi Arabia), of a typical diameter of 18 mm and a concentric layup, composed of wires of an
15   equal diameter (Table S8). The cables’ properties are assessed from their specifications in terms
     of total linear net weight (kg/km), breaking force (kN), and DC resistance at 20 °C (Ohm/km).
           For comparison, we provide an estimate of an all-CNT based overhead power cable made of
     intercalated CNTIC fibers with the properties measured in this study. Due to high mechanical
     properties, such a conceptual cable would require neither an additional reinforcing core, nor an
20   additional aluminum encapsulation or conducting shell, in contrast to some novel aluminum
     conductor composite core (ACCC)-types of cables with a carbon fiber composite core, where
     aluminum encapsulation and /or shell is still required to ensure conductivity. The CNTIC cable
     considered is of the same diameter as conventional ACSR (18 mm), assuming a perfect hexagonal
     packing of individual CNTIC filaments (wires), which translates into ~241 k wires necessary to
25   produce such a diameter.
           With volumetric density of 1409 kg/m3, such CNTIC cable would have a net weight of about
     295 kg per kilometer, which is ~2.5 times lower than conventional ACSR available on the market,
     offering a large weight reduction. The breaking load of the CNTIC cable of 18 mm diameter would
     be ~349 kN, which is 5 times stronger than a conventional ACSR (~70 kN), while offering same
30   level of electrical conductivity. Data can be found in Table S7.
